01

Executive Summary and Group Overview

REvil, tracked in the antivirus industry as Sodinokibi (abbreviated Sodin), was one of the most prolific and financially damaging ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) operations of the 2019 to 2021 period. The malware first appeared on April 17, 2019, deployed against Oracle WebLogic servers through CVE-2019-2725, and reached its peak with the July 2021 Kaseya VSA supply-chain attack, which pushed ransomware to roughly 1,500 downstream businesses simultaneously behind a 70 million US dollar universal-decryptor demand.

The operation is assessed as a direct continuation of GandCrab (Secureworks GOLD GARDEN) by the same or a substantially overlapping core developer set. REvil ran a closed, vetted affiliate program with an advertised 70/30 split, pioneered systematized double extortion, and from June 2020 added data auctions. As of July 2026 it is effectively defunct following FBI-led technical disruption in 2021 and Russian FSB arrests in January 2022, with legal proceedings concluding in Russia across 2024 and 2025.

02

Lineage and Organizational Heritage

Assessed Connection to GandCrab
Confirmed: four vendors independently corroborate

The prevailing cross-vendor assessment is that REvil's core developers are the same individuals, or a substantially overlapping set, who operated the GandCrab RaaS (Secureworks GOLD GARDEN). GandCrab was among the most prolific operations of 2018 to 2019, with operator-claimed revenue exceeding 2 billion US dollars before its announced retirement on May 31, 2019. REvil emerged two months earlier and accelerated immediately afterward. Secureworks, CrowdStrike, McAfee ATR, and Sophos independently corroborate the lineage; Brian Krebs first posited it publicly in June 2019.

Vendor framing nuance: CrowdStrike frames the transition as a single actor evolving from GandCrab into REvil (the PINCHY SPIDER adversary). Secureworks frames it more narrowly as GOLD GARDEN transferring the GandCrab source code to GOLD SOUTHFIELD in April 2019. Both support continuity; the distinction (identical personnel versus a source-code handoff between related groups) is not fully resolved in open sources.
Evidentiary Pillars
Pillar 1: High
Code Overlap
Secureworks CTU identified a near-identical string-decoding function shared by GandCrab and REvil. URL-building logic was specific enough that reproducing it would require access to GandCrab source code.
Pillar 2: High
Beta Code Artifacts
Early REvil beta samples contained strings "gcfin" (assessed: GandCrab Final) and "gc6" (GandCrab 6), suggesting REvil began development as GandCrab version 6 before rebranding.
Pillar 3: High
Timing Correlation
REvil shipped as an additional payload alongside GandCrab on April 17, 2019. GandCrab retired May 31, 2019. REvil activity accelerated and expanded delivery methods immediately post-retirement.
Pillar 4: Moderate
CIS Exclusion Fingerprint
Both implement near-identical keyboard/locale checks to avoid CIS hosts. A common practice in isolation, but the implementation specifics match, reinforcing the other pillars.
Vendor Designation Disambiguation

GOLD GARDEN (Secureworks) designates the GandCrab operators. GOLD SOUTHFIELD (Secureworks) and PINCHY SPIDER (CrowdStrike) designate the REvil operators; MITRE tracks the group as G0115. Microsoft tracks the malware family as Ransom:Win32/Sodinokibi. Unit 42 and Recorded Future track by the common name REvil / Sodinokibi without a distinct group codename. These are operator-group designations and should not be conflated with malware-family names.

03

Operational Model

RaaS Structure

REvil operated a closed, vetted RaaS with a limited-partnership affiliate model. The core GOLD SOUTHFIELD / PINCHY SPIDER team controlled malware development, backend infrastructure, payment processing, and the leak site; affiliates conducted intrusions, deployed the ransomware, and handled initial victim communications. CrowdStrike characterizes this as a "partnership program with a limited number of accounts," distinct from open-recruitment RaaS. Affiliates were recruited on high-tier Russian-language forums (XSS and Exploit) requiring reputation and vetting.

Revenue Split and the Affiliate Backdoor
Credible: AdvIntel / Flashpoint, corroborated by forum disputes

The standard advertised split was 70/30 in favor of the affiliate. AdvIntel and Flashpoint research (September 2021) revealed a cryptographic backdoor in affiliate builds that let core operators hijack victim negotiations, pose as the victim abandoning talks (the "double chat" technique), then resume directly and collect the full ransom, cutting the affiliate out.

Structural weakness: An affiliate publicly sought 21.5 million US dollars in stolen commissions from REvil on the Exploit forum in May 2021. LockBit representatives later stated former REvil affiliates confirmed being defrauded. The revelation left REvil having victimized both external targets and its own criminal partners, a durable reputational liability. The April 2022 reappearance advertised an improved 80/20 split, plausibly to rebuild affiliate trust.

Double and Triple Extortion

REvil systematized double extortion: data exfiltration prior to encryption, then threatened publication on the "Happy Blog" (a deliberately taunting name). From June 2020 it introduced data auctions, offering stolen data to third-party buyers if the victim refused to pay, a primitive third-extortion vector. In the Grubman Shire Meiselas & Sacks breach, operators threatened to sell data to political opponents of then-President Trump, a high-visibility leverage threat that ultimately did not materialize.

Encryption Scheme

Hybrid design: Salsa20 symmetric stream cipher for file content, Curve25519 (elliptic-curve Diffie-Hellman) for key encapsulation, with SHA-3 and CRC32 hashing and RC4 for runtime string decryption. API resolution uses 32-bit hashes for anti-analysis, and I/O completion ports enable fast asynchronous multithreaded encryption. Shadow copies are deleted via PowerShell/WMI (legacy vssadmin on Windows XP).

Backdoor foundation: Each file is encrypted with a unique key derived from a victim-specific public key, but the attacker's master public key is embedded in every binary. This let the REvil core decrypt any victim independently of affiliates, the cryptographic basis of the double-chat backdoor.
CIS Exclusion Behavior
Confirmed: Malpedia, Intel 471

REvil implements a CIS keyboard/locale kill switch using GetKeyboardLayoutList, GetUserDefaultUILanguage, and GetSystemDefaultUILanguage. On Russian or other CIS-region layouts or languages, the malware halts without encrypting. This is a deliberate operational-security and legal safe-harbor mechanism consistent with operation from Russian Federation territory, present from early versions.

Decryptor Availability
Confirmed: Bitdefender, No More Ransom

A Bitdefender universal decryptor (released September 2021 with an undisclosed law enforcement partner) recovers files for all REvil victims encrypted before July 13, 2021, and is distributed free via Bitdefender and the No More Ransom project. By November 2021 more than 1,400 companies in 83 countries had used it, saving an estimated 550 million US dollars. Some later variants are not decryptable. Separately, a REvil developer accidentally generated a master key during the Kaseya response; the FBI obtained it and shared it with Kaseya on July 21, 2021. The Washington Post reported the FBI had secretly accessed REvil servers weeks earlier and withheld keys to protect a planned operation that was never executed because REvil disappeared first.

Linux / ESXi Variant

REvix, a Linux and ESXi variant, was released in June 2021, extending REvil to virtualized infrastructure and VMware environments. This capability was later continued by Ransom Cartel, which used DonPAPI to harvest vCenter credentials before encrypting ESXi-associated files (.log, .vmdk, .vmem, .vswp, .vmsn).

Financial Infrastructure

Payment Model

REvil accepted both Bitcoin and Monero, with a stated preference for Monero (ring signatures, stealth addresses, RingCT). Bitcoin carried a 10% surcharge reflecting higher tracing risk. The Kaseya 70 million US dollar demand notably requested Bitcoin; Elliptic's Tom Robinson attributed this to the difficulty of acquiring that sum in Monero on available exchanges. OFAC confirmed REvil received more than 200 million US dollars in ransom paid in Bitcoin and Monero across documented incidents.

On-Chain Laundering Methodology (Elliptic)
1
Layering
Ransom payments split across multiple wallets and mixed with Bitcoin from other sources to break the direct link to the victim payment.
2
Transaction Chaining
Funds passed through many intermediate addresses to obscure the chain of custody before reaching cash-out points.
3
Phased Cash-Out
Laundering remained active weeks after payment in documented cases, indicating a deliberate, staged approach through complicit exchanges.
Credible: Elliptic is the primary REvil-specific blockchain-forensics source

Elliptic published the most detailed REvil-specific tracing (payment lifecycle, layering, exchange mapping). Chainalysis and TRM Labs cover REvil within general ransomware tracking, but no equally prominent REvil-dedicated public case study from those firms was identified.

Victim Profile and Targeting

Scale and Sector Profile

REvil ransomware was deployed on approximately 175,000 computers worldwide (OFAC). IBM placed REvil's 2020 profits alone at roughly 123 million US dollars (conservative). Small businesses are estimated to have made up 50 to 75% of victims (Elliptic), handled by affiliates seeking reliable smaller payments, while high-value "big game" targets generated the headline demands. Targeted sectors span technology and MSPs, food and agriculture, legal services, financial services and travel, healthcare, manufacturing and technology hardware, government, and education. Spokesperson UNKN publicly named agriculture as a deliberate future target in October 2020.

Geography and Exclusions
  • Focus: predominantly the United States, Western Europe, Australia, and Canada; the Kaseya attack affected victims in at least 17 countries.
  • CIS exclusion: keyboard/locale kill switch prevents execution on Russian and CIS systems.
  • Operational whitelisting: system directories and specific files excluded to preserve negotiability; database, backup, and security processes killed via the prc configuration key.

Law Enforcement and Regulatory Response

U.S. Indictments and Sentencing

Yaroslav Vasinskyi ("Rabotnik"), Ukrainian national: arrested in Poland on October 8, 2021; 11-count indictment unsealed November 8, 2021 (Northern District of Texas); extradited March 2022; pleaded guilty August 2022. Sentenced May 1, 2024 to 13 years and 7 months and ordered to pay 16 million US dollars in restitution for 2,500+ attacks demanding over 700 million US dollars.

Yevgeniy Polyanin, Russian national: indicted November 8, 2021 on the same counts (maximum exposure 145 years); 6.1 million US dollars seized from his FTX account. Remains a fugitive on the FBI Cyber Most Wanted list (believed to be in Barnaul, Russia) as of March 2026; Russian citizenship makes extradition highly unlikely.

Multinational Arrests and FSB Action

Under Operation GoldDust (17 countries, Europol, Eurojust, INTERPOL), authorities arrested five Sodinokibi/REvil affiliates and two GandCrab suspects. Two REvil affiliates were arrested in Romania on November 4, 2021. On January 14, 2022 the Russian FSB, acting on a U.S. request, raided 25 locations across Moscow, St. Petersburg, and surrounding oblasts, detaining 14 individuals.

Charge narrowing: Russian charges were limited to illegal circulation of means of payment and use/distribution of malware. The indictment excluded the major ransomware episodes (JBS, Kaseya, Acer): defendants were effectively charged with card fraud against Americans, reflecting the breakdown in U.S.-Russia cooperation after the Ukraine invasion.

  • U.S. State Department: up to 10 million US dollars for information on key REvil leadership; up to 5 million for information leading to arrest/conviction of any participant.

Attribution and State Nexus

Assessment
Confirmed: no directed state nexus established

No direct nexus to Russian state intelligence (FSB, SVR, or GRU) has been established by any government authority or major vendor. REvil is consistently assessed as a financially motivated criminal organization operating under implicit Russian state tolerance rather than as a directed state asset. The "safe harbor" framing (criminals operating freely from Russian territory with implicit non-interference) is well supported; active state tasking is not confirmed.

Trajectory Assessment

Disappearance and Return Cycles
  • Jul 13, 2021: first disappearance, triggered by FBI infrastructure compromise.
  • Sep 2021: Happy Blog and payment portals restored; operations briefly resumed.
  • Late Oct 2021: second disappearance after the Vasinskyi arrest and multinational pressure.
  • Apr 2022: anomalous Happy Blog reappearance (Oil India content; 80/20 "join us" page); did not develop into a sustained operation. Actor attribution never publicly confirmed.

Single-source flag (Ransom Cartel): the linkage rests on a single primary technical source (Unit 42, with MalwareHunterTeam's initial identification). No corroborating second-source technical assessment was identified, and neither Mandiant nor Recorded Future has published a formal Ransom Cartel to REvil equivalence assessment. Trajectory Indicators and Intelligence Gaps
  • Trajectory: rising sharply through H1 2021 (up to 42% of known victims post-Kaseya), then abrupt collapse. No voluntary wind-down signals; REvil was at its commercial peak when disrupted.
  • Definitive exit: the January 2022 FSB raids; the FSB stated the gang "ceased to exist." No credible reconstitution under the REvil name has been confirmed since.
  • Gaps: the UNKN spokesperson and principal developers remain publicly unidentified; the FSB-named "ringleader" was never publicly named; Polyanin remains at large; the full Ermakov/Medibank-to-REvil network relationship is undocumented.
