01

Executive Summary and Entity Overview

Overall Assessment
Credible

Dyre (also Dyreza; component names Dyzap and Dyranges) is the connective-tissue node between the GameOver Zeus era and the TrickBot, Ryuk, and Conti cluster. First identified by the Dell SecureWorks Counter Threat Unit in early June 2014, within days of the June 2 Operation Tovar takedown of GameOver Zeus, Dyre filled the vacuum that action created and grew over seventeen months into one of the most prevalent and technically advanced banking trojans in the wild.

Two features define it analytically. First, Dyre was run by a closed operator gang rather than sold as a kit, a structural contrast with Zeus and SpyEye, and Symantec's command-and-control telemetry (roughly 285 servers, a five-day working week on Eastern Europe / Russia hours) is the basis for its reputation as a group that operated "like a business." Second, Dyre ended not through Western law enforcement but through an anomalous Russian domestic action: all spam campaigns ceased abruptly on November 18, 2015, correlating with a reported November 2015 raid on a Moscow film company. Russia acting against its own cybercriminals inverts the Bogachev safe-harbor pattern and is the profile's central open question.

The lineage is defunct only in name. The Dyre group is assessed to have re-formed as TrickBot in 2016, carrying its tooling and at least some personnel into the operation that would go on to enable Ryuk and Conti. That handoff makes Dyre the missing root of the TrickBot spine already in this library.

02

Lineage and Heritage

Emergence in the GameOver Zeus Vacuum
Confirmed

The temporal relationship is well corroborated: GameOver Zeus and CryptoLocker were dismantled via Operation Tovar on June 2, 2014, with Bogachev indicted the same day, and Dyre was first identified by Dell SecureWorks in early June 2014, within the same window. This proximity is the basis for treating Dyre as an ecosystem-regeneration event. No source proves Dyre's operators were displaced GameOver Zeus personnel; the link is inferential, based on timing and the general pattern of a criminal ecosystem refilling after enforcement.

Fresh Codebase Versus Zeus Derivative
Credible

Sources disagree, and the disagreement is a code-versus-crew conflation worth preserving. CSIS (the June 2014 discoverer) framed Dyre as a distinct, purpose-built family rather than a Zeus derivative; Cisco Talos treats it as its own banking-malware family with Zeus cited only as a behavioral comparator. Reuters' 2016 law-enforcement sources called Dyre "related to Zeus," but that reflects operational and personnel ties to the Bogachev milieu, not shared source code. F5 Labs hedges that "no official attribution to the source code can be confirmed."

Assessment: The technical-researcher consensus is that Dyre is architecturally distinct from Zeus, a custom man-in-the-middle proxy trojan rather than a leaked-source fork, while being operationally connected to the Zeus/GameOver Zeus criminal world. Confidence: moderate-to-high on distinct code, high on operational relationship. No researcher has produced source-code lineage proving a fork.
The GOLD EVERGREEN Thread
Credible

Secureworks provides the most concrete vendor link between Dyre and the earlier Bogachev enterprise. It does not give Dyre its own actor codename; instead it assesses that GOLD EVERGREEN, its designation for the GameOver Zeus group, "diversified its malware portfolio using the Dyre and Bugat v5 (Dridex) trojans," and that "some Dyre attacks could be connected to the GOLD EVERGREEN threat group." Secureworks is careful to add that there is "insufficient information to determine if GOLD EVERGREEN is using TrickBot," so it does not collapse Dyre and TrickBot into one label. This thread strengthens the inference of personnel continuity from the GameOver Zeus milieu into Dyre, without confirming it.

03

Operational and Business Model

A Closed Operator Gang

Dyre is consistently characterized as a closed, non-affiliate operator group rather than a malware-as-a-service kit, a structural contrast with Zeus and SpyEye, which were commercialized to many independent operators. IBM's April 2015 Dyre Wolf report described "an experienced and resource-backed cybercrime gang" running Dyre directly rather than licensing it, while noting the code could be operated by several connected teams attacking different geographies, suggesting internal compartmentalization rather than open-market distribution.

"Run Like a Business"
Credible

The strongest evidentiary basis for the group's business-like structure is Symantec's June 2015 command-and-control telemetry: roughly 285 C2 servers, and an operating rhythm in which "the attackers appear to adhere to a five-day working week, with no activity on Saturday and Sunday," most activity from 09:00 to 16:00 UTC, consistent with UTC+2 / UTC+3 (Eastern Europe / Russia) time zones. This is methodologically strong but effectively single-source (Symantec), and no direct organizational documents (payroll, org charts, office material) surfaced for the Dyre period, unlike later leaked-chat ransomware groups.

Distribution and Cash-Out
  • Delivery: the Upatre downloader in spam disguised as invoices, faxes, and delivery notices, amplified by the Cutwail spambot; a later worm variant abused victims' own Outlook clients to spread Upatre.
  • Money mules: CSIS's original June 2014 reporting found a tailored money-mule interface in Dyre's C2 tied to accounts in Riga, Latvia, where recruits temporarily held stolen funds before onward transfer (single-source, CSIS).
  • Dyre Wolf cash-out: after fraudulent wires, funds were moved rapidly between banks in smaller tranches, and DDoS attacks were launched against the victim organization specifically to delay detection and recovery.
04

Technical Capabilities

Browser Hooking and MITM Interception

Dyre hooked all three major browsers, hooking PR_Read / PR_Write in nspr4.dll for Firefox, the SSL functions in Chrome, and wininet.dll for Internet Explorer, and used a dedicated man-in-the-middle proxy rather than simple form-grabbing. Targeted-bank traffic was routed through Dyre's proxy, which served fabricated login pages while relaying the victim's real credentials to the bank to complete an apparently normal login, letting the attacker log in as the victim in parallel. This design bypassed in-browser SSL/TLS to capture plaintext credentials and defeat two-factor authentication.

Persistence, Remote Access, and Anti-Analysis
  • Persistence: registration as a fake "Google Update Service" plus a scheduled task, with injection into svchost.exe or Explorer.exe depending on OS version.
  • Hidden VNC: a built-in remote-access module for interactive control during high-value corporate account takeovers (well reported; not re-confirmed against a single primary this pass).
  • Anti-analysis (MITRE S0024): sandbox and VM detection via process-list and registry checks, packed/encrypted resources, plus (per Proofpoint) mouse-movement and screen-resolution checks, timing delays, JavaScript obfuscation, and per-sample binary randomization to defeat hash detection.
C2 Resilience: SSL, DGA, and I2P

Dyre protected command-and-control with SSL and RSA-signed configurations, and by early 2015 added a domain-generation algorithm fallback and, notably, an I2P (Invisible Internet Project) fallback, an anonymity-network capability unusual among contemporaneous banking trojans (Palo Alto Unit 42, February 2015). DGA domain-count figures differ across sources and versions: Cisco Talos reported roughly 333 domains per day, while Unit 42 and Secureworks/Sophos reported about 1,000 per day. This reflects version and measurement differences, not a single resolvable number.

The Dyre Wolf Campaign
Confirmed

IBM's April 2015 report is the definitive primary source on the group's most distinctive tactic. Corporate victims were infected via spear-phishing; when the victim tried to reach one of hundreds of pre-configured bank URLs, a fake "site experiencing issues, please call this number" page appeared; the victim called and reached a live English-speaking operator posing as bank support who extracted username, password, and two-factor codes, sometimes asking to loop in a co-worker with equivalent access to bypass dual-authorization controls; high-value wire transfers followed (reported at $500,000 to $1.5 million per incident); and for high-value targets a volumetric DDoS was launched to delay detection. IBM called this social-engineering-plus-DDoS combination unprecedented for a banking-trojan fraud scheme.

CIS / Russian-Language Behavior
Analyst Inference

Evidence is inferential rather than a confirmed hard-coded kill switch. Symantec's C2 telemetry noted that a large share of Dyre's infrastructure sat in Eastern Europe / Russia while relatively few infections and no targeted financial institutions were seen there, and speculated the operators "may be reluctant to draw attention." No source located a reverse-engineered Russian-keyboard-layout or locale-check kill switch in Dyre's binary, so the CIS-exclusion pattern rests on target-list and C2-geography inference, not code evidence. This is an intelligence gap.

06

Financial Infrastructure

Wire Fraud, Not Crypto

Dyre's monetization was overwhelmingly wire-fraud-based. Stolen credentials and, in the Dyre Wolf phase, live-social-engineered two-factor codes authorized large transfers from consumer and increasingly corporate accounts to attacker-controlled offshore accounts, with funds then moved rapidly between banks in smaller tranches to complicate tracing. Dyre predates the ransomware-era cryptocurrency laundering ecosystem: no Bitcoin or Monero infrastructure specific to Dyre is documented, and no blockchain-forensics firm (Chainalysis, Elliptic, TRM Labs) or OFAC wallet action targets Dyre, which is expected given its fiat model and should not be read as a sourcing failure.

07

Victim Profile and Targeting

Targets and Geography

Initial 2014 targeting focused on UK and US retail-banking customers (Bank of America, Citibank, NatWest, RBS, Ulster Bank). By early 2015 the target list expanded across UK high-street banks (Barclays, HSBC, Lloyds, Santander) and into Germany, Australia, and France. Trend Micro's January 2015 telemetry placed 68% of infections in the United States, 10% in Canada, and 4% in Chile. Reuters named Bank of America and JPMorgan Chase among affected US institutions, targeting their account holders rather than bank infrastructure directly.

Target-List Growth and the Corporate Pivot

The target list grew from roughly 206 sites in the pre-2015 configuration to 355 by early 2015, with Dell SecureWorks describing manipulation of communications with more than 400 institutions and later retrospectives citing over 1,000 spoofed sites cumulatively. Dyre began as consumer retail-banking credential theft and evolved by early-to-mid 2015 into a dual-track model adding high-value corporate targeting through the Dyre Wolf campaign. IBM's John Kuhn noted the unusual focus: "I don't know of another campaign or Trojan that targets corporations and not individuals." A later 2015 variant also began targeting Bitcoin wallet sites in its web-inject configuration.

08

Law Enforcement and Regulatory Response

The November 2015 Russian Raid
Credible

Reuters' February 6, 2016 exclusive (Joseph Menn) is the foundational and essentially only primary journalistic account, sourced to three contacts "with knowledge of the matter." The raid reportedly occurred in November 2015 at the Moscow offices of 25th Floor, a film distribution and production company in the Moscow-City complex. Reuters explicitly stated it "was unable to establish a direct connection between the halt of the malware's activities and the raid itself." Symantec independently confirmed that all Dyre spam campaigns ceased around November 18, 2015 and never resumed, distinguishing this from a routine operational pause (contrasting it with a Dridex pause that did not represent termination).

The "Botnet" Film and the Kovalev Link

25th Floor was reportedly producing a cybercrime thriller titled "Botnet," loosely based on a 2010 US mule-network case. Later reporting (Krebs on Security, February 2023, citing a 2016 Forbes.ru story) identified Vitaly Kovalev as the figure behind the film project and reported sources describing the studio as a money-laundering front for the Dyre crew.

Confidence note: The Kovalev-as-filmmaker attribution and the money-laundering-front claim rest on a single source chain (Krebs relaying Forbes.ru). The contemporaneous Russian coverage (xakep.ru, 2016) confirms the 25th Floor raid and the "Botnet" film but does not name Kovalev. A separate claim that Kovalev was personally detained in 2016 under Russian Criminal Code Article 273 and the case quietly closed could not be corroborated in any located source and is therefore not carried in this profile.
Arrests, Official Posture, and the US Gap
  • Arrests: thin and contradictory. Forbes (2016) reported roughly 50 arrests with "most released" (its own text noted no evidence anyone at the film company had been charged over Dyre). No named, formally charged Dyre-specific defendant has been confirmed by any government.
  • Official posture: the FSB declined to comment; the Interior Ministry denied departmental involvement (implying a different agency acted). Neither confirmed the raid's purpose.
  • US response: a well-corroborated negative. No US indictment or OFAC action was ever issued specifically for Dyre. Kovalev's only US charge is a 2012 New Jersey money-mule indictment that predates Dyre.
Contrast With Operation Tovar

The asymmetry is itself analytically significant. Operation Tovar (June 2014) was a coordinated, multinational, publicly documented action with unsealed charges against a named individual, court-authorized technical measures, and international coordination. The Dyre disruption has never been officially confirmed by any government, involved a domestic Russian raid on a film company rather than a malware-infrastructure takedown, and produced no public court filings, no named Dyre-specific defendant, and no coordinated international statement.

Attribution and State Nexus

The Central Puzzle: An Inverted Pattern

Why would Russian authorities act against Dyre's operators when Russia has a documented pattern of tolerating cybercriminals who avoid domestic targets, the Bogachev safe-harbor pattern? By the available targeting evidence, Dyre observed the conventional non-Russian-targeting norm that usually earns tolerance, which makes the apparent Russian-initiated action against it an inversion rather than an application of the usual rule.

Competing Explanations
Hypothesis A • Credible
Ordinary Prosecution
A genuine, if opaque, law-enforcement action, described by Reuters' experts as possibly Russia's biggest cybercrime crackdown to date. Weakness: no source identifies a domestic-Russian victim or a specific triggering incident that would explain why Dyre crossed a line others did not.
Hypothesis B • Inference
FSB Score-Settling / Asset Seizure
A film company serving as a laundering front, the total absence of formal charges, and later non-prosecution are consistent with an informal internal action (a faction dispute, an intelligence unit appropriating proceeds, or removing a liability). No source makes this claim directly; it is a plausible but unconfirmed hypothesis.
Hypothesis C • Weak
Crossed a Domestic Line
Symantec found Russian and Eastern European institutions were generally not on Dyre's target list, so the group appears to have observed the non-targeting norm. This weakens a domestic-targeting explanation as a standalone cause.
Baseline
The Bogachev Comparator
Bogachev's apparent protection was contingent on usefulness (a "carrot" model). Dyre represents the state acting against, rather than for, a compliant criminal group, the least-resolved feature of the case.
Assigned Nexus Tier
Analyst Inference

Tolerated safe harbor, terminated by an unexplained internal or state action. This is distinct from "no nexus" (a state action did occur, just unexplained) and from "probable cooperation" or "direct control" (no evidence Dyre was tasked with state objectives during its operational life). Supporting some state involvement: the FSB's no-comment posture, the Interior Ministry's denial implying a different agency acted, and the later 2020/2023 OFAC finding that the successor TrickBot organization had a Russian-intelligence-services association, which raises but does not answer whether that relationship began during or after the Dyre-era disruption.

Dating question for the analyst: Whether the FSB-TrickBot intelligence relationship documented by OFAC in 2020 and 2023 existed in any form during the Dyre period (2014 to 2015), or developed only after the 2015 to 2016 disruption and regrouping, bears directly on how far back the state-nexus inversion should be dated. This is an open collection requirement.

Trajectory and Legacy Assessment

Dyre as the Bridge Node

The arc is the profile's core contribution: GameOver Zeus disrupted June 2014, Dyre emerges within days and operates as a closed-shop banking-fraud gang for seventeen months, Dyre is disrupted by an opaque Russian domestic action in November 2015, the same tooling lineage and some of the same personnel re-emerge as TrickBot in September 2016, and TrickBot then evolves into the ransomware-enabling platform underpinning Ryuk (2018) and Conti (2020). Dyre demonstrates the ecosystem's defining property: enforcement disruptions do not eliminate criminal capability, they redistribute and rebrand it, typically with a six-to-twelve-month lag and continuity of core personnel even where the malware code is substantially rewritten.

  • State-nexus timing: whether the FSB-TrickBot relationship existed during the Dyre period is unknown and directly affects how the nexus inversion is dated.
