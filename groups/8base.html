01

Executive Summary and Group Overview

8Base was a high-volume, financially motivated ransomware operation that functioned as a registered affiliate of the Phobos Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) ecosystem, designated "Affiliate 2803" in the unsealed U.S. Department of Justice charging documents. It deployed a Phobos variant using the .8base file extension and ran full double extortion: data was exfiltrated before encryption, then victims were pressured through a darknet leak site and a Telegram channel. The group branded itself as "simple pen testers" punishing organizations that neglected security, a moral framing common to extortion crews.

8Base first drew wide attention in a May–June 2023 surge, when it ranked as the second most active ransomware brand globally in May 2023 (67 confirmed victims that month, behind only LockBit). It sustained high tempo through late 2024. On February 10, 2025, Operation Phobos Aetor seized its darknet infrastructure and led to four arrests in Phuket, Thailand. In July 2025 Japan's National Police Agency released a free decryptor covering Phobos and 8Base. As of July 2026 there is no confirmed reemergence or rebranding.

Overall assessment (ANALYST INFERENCE, high confidence): 8Base is effectively disrupted. Core operators are in custody, the Phobos administrator has pleaded guilty, the extortion infrastructure is seized, and a functional free decryptor neutralizes the group's leverage over historic victims. Residual risk lies in operator recidivism and the wider Phobos affiliate pool rather than in 8Base as a standing brand. Monitoring for reconstitution under a new alias remains warranted.

Lineage and Organizational Heritage

8Base sits at the intersection of three candidate lineage relationships, each carrying different evidentiary weight. The Phobos affiliation is prosecutorially established; the RansomHouse resemblance is a design-similarity observation; the "experienced actor rebranding" thesis is circumstantial.

Pillar 1 — Confirmed
Phobos Affiliate (Prosecutorial Finding)
DOJ charging documents (Feb 10, 2025) state that Berezhnoy, Glebov, and others "operated a ransomware affiliate organization, including under the names '8Base' and 'Affiliate 2803,'... through the deployment of Phobos ransomware." Europol confirmed 8Base "took advantage of Phobos' infrastructure" and developed its own variant using Phobos encryption and delivery.
Pillar 2 — Confirmed (binary forensics)
Zero Code Divergence from Phobos
Cisco Talos (Nov 2023) compared the 8Base Phobos sample against prior Phobos variants and found "no differences between the code at the binary level at all" — only configuration data differed. VMware Carbon Black first observed Phobos samples using the .8base extension. Single strong primary source (Talos), corroborated by extension observation.
Pillar 3 — Low-Medium
RansomHouse Resemblance
VMware Carbon Black (Jun 28, 2023) reported a ~99% match between 8Base and RansomHouse ransom notes and leak-site welcome copy "copied word for word." No code, infrastructure, or personnel overlap has been confirmed. VMware itself did not conclude they are the same group; the tie remains uncorroborated.
Pillar 4 — Medium (circumstantial)
Pre-existing Actor Continuity
VMware assessed 8Base's speed and maturity signalled "a well-established mature organization," not a new group. Early sample compile timestamps (Jun 2022) predate public activity by ~1 year, and DOJ dates operator activity to May 2019 — supporting the view that Berezhnoy and Glebov were long-active actors before the 8Base brand.
Phobos Family Heritage

Phobos is an evolution of the CrySiS / Dharma ransomware family. CrySiS emerged in 2016; Dharma followed when CrySiS decryptors appeared; Phobos emerged around October–December 2018 as a further evolution once Dharma decryptors became available (documented by Coveware in January 2019 and Europol in December 2018). Phobos and Dharma share near-identical ransom-note text and file-naming conventions. Cisco Talos established that the same RSA public key protected the encryption key across all Phobos samples analyzed since 2019, confirming a shared builder and enabling universal decryption once the corresponding private key was recovered (see Section 04).

Vendor Designation Disambiguation
Rule applied: the Phobos-family cluster and the 8Base affiliate are tracked under separate designations. Do not conflate a Phobos-ecosystem label with an 8Base-specific one. Palo Alto Unit 42 tracks 8Base as Squalid Scorpius and the broader Phobos family separately as Weary Scorpius.

03

Operational Model

Phobos RaaS Structure

8Base operated as Affiliate 2803 within the Phobos network. Under the Phobos model, the administrator (Evgenii Ptitsyn, monikers "derxan" and "zimmermanx") sold access to the builder and maintained coordination infrastructure and cryptocurrency wallets. Affiliates purchased builder access, conducted intrusions independently, and paid a per-decryption-key fee to the administrator after each successful attack. Each deployment was assigned a unique alphanumeric string matched to a decryption key. Affiliates received ransoms in affiliate-specific wallets, which then transferred key fees to Ptitsyn-controlled wallets (documented flow December 2021–April 2024).

Analyst Inference

The FBI alleged Berezhnoy promoted the ransomware on cybercrime forums to recruit "long-term" affiliates, suggesting 8Base itself may have maintained a sub-affiliate or operational support layer. The exact revenue split is not disclosed in court documents; standard Phobos arrangements had affiliates keep the bulk of the ransom while remitting a per-key fee to the administrator.

Double Extortion Sequence
  • Exfiltration: data stolen prior to encryption using RClone, WinSCP, and Mega.io.
  • Encryption: Phobos payload deployed with the .8base extension.
  • Leak threat: victim posted to the darknet leak site with a countdown timer; data published if unpaid.
  • Direct contact: operators reached out to victims directly to open negotiations, in addition to on-host ransom notes.

No documented evidence of triple extortion (DDoS or customer-notification threats) appears in available sourcing.

Negotiation Behavior
  • Demand range: hundreds of thousands to millions of dollars; exact per-victim ranges not uniformly documented (CREDIBLE).
  • Deadlines: countdown timers on the leak site with threat of public data release.
  • Terms: victims could negotiate for both decryption keys and deletion assurances; NCC Group noted "very specific terms of service" prohibiting third-party involvement in negotiations.
  • Communication: initial contact via info.hta / info.txt; ongoing negotiation via operator email embedded in the file extension (e.g., [support@rexsdata.pro].8base) and via the Telegram channel.

Encryption Implementation
  • Algorithm: AES-256-CBC (custom implementation, not the Windows Crypto API since the post-2019 change).
  • Per-file key: a unique random AES key per file, encrypted with a hardcoded RSA-1024 public key and appended to each file.
  • Speed optimization: full encryption below 1.5 MB; partial block encryption above 1.5 MB, with encrypted-block lists saved in file metadata.
  • File extension: <filename>.id[<VolumeSerial>-<ID>].[<operator email>].8base
  • Config: 70+ options, AES-encrypted with a hardcoded key, including a blocklist that avoids re-encrypting .8base and ~80+ other Phobos variant extensions — effectively mapping the Phobos affiliate ecosystem.
CIS Exclusion Behavior
Confirmed — Cyrillic language check present in binary

Cisco Talos confirmed the Phobos binary used by 8Base checks for Cyrillic OS language settings to "prevent the malware from running in unwanted environments." HHS HC3 (Nov 2023) stated no attacks were reported against former Soviet or CIS countries, corroborated by BleepingComputer. The combination of Cyrillic kill switch and zero documented CIS victims is consistent with standard Russia-nexus criminal behavior, though no confirmed intelligence-service nexus exists (see Section 08).

Free Decryptor (July 2025)
CONFIRMED: On July 18, 2025, Japan's National Police Agency released a free decryptor for Phobos and 8Base, publicized with Europol and the FBI and distributed via the No More Ransom platform. It supports .phobos, .8base, .elbie, .faust, and .LIZARD (with additional extensions possible). BleepingComputer tested it and recovered all 150 test files encrypted by the LIZARD variant.

Decryption is feasible because the same RSA public key is reused across all Phobos variants since 2019, so recovery of the corresponding private key — likely obtained through the law-enforcement operation and Ptitsyn's arrest — enables decryption across samples. No public decryptor existed for post-2019 Phobos variants before July 2025. No confirmed Linux or ESXi variant of 8Base is documented; it was a Windows-focused operation.

Payment and Wallet Structure

Payment was exclusively in Bitcoin across all documented sourcing. DOJ places total extortion at approximately $16 million across the Phobos/8Base operation (May 2019–October 2024). Each affiliate received ransoms in a unique wallet; per-decryption-key fees then flowed to Ptitsyn-controlled wallets. Thai authorities seized digital wallets from the four arrested individuals in February 2025.

Laundering Methodology
Credible

Europol, Thai police reporting, and BleepingComputer describe the use of cryptocurrency mixing services to obscure transaction trails; suspects are accused of "laundering payments through digital mixing platforms." No specific mixer services are named in open-source reporting, and no use of bridges or named cash-out exchanges is documented.

Intelligence gap: no public-facing Chainalysis, TRM Labs, or Elliptic report specifically naming 8Base wallet addresses was identified. Law-enforcement forensics are documented, but firm-published on-chain analysis of 8Base is not available in open source.
Sanctions Context

No OFAC sanctions have been confirmed against 8Base actors or wallets. On the same day as the 8Base takedown (February 10, 2025), OFAC sanctioned Zservers, a Russia-based bulletproof hosting provider used by LockBit affiliates — a coordinated but separate action not directed at 8Base infrastructure.

08

Law Enforcement and Regulatory Response

Operation Phobos Aetor (February 10, 2025)

An international operation seized 8Base's darknet leak and negotiation sites and led to four arrests in Phuket, Thailand. The site seizure was executed by the Bavarian State Criminal Police Office on behalf of the Bamberg Public Prosecutor. Europol's operation took down 27 servers; the broader U.S.-cited disruption references more than 100 servers linked to the Phobos network. Roughly 40 items (laptops, phones, digital wallets) were seized, and over 400 companies worldwide were warned of ongoing or imminent attacks. The operation was coordinated by Europol and Eurojust across some 14 countries (including Germany, Thailand, USA, UK, Switzerland, Japan, Romania, Spain, France, Belgium, Czechia).

  • Italy, 2023: an unnamed "key Phobos affiliate involved in development and distribution" was arrested on a French warrant (Europol); identity not disclosed.

Attribution and State Nexus

Assessment: Criminal Financial Motivation, No Confirmed State Nexus
Analyst Inference — no confirmed RIS nexus

No government, law-enforcement, or threat-intelligence vendor has established a connection between 8Base and Russian state intelligence (FSB, SVR, GRU) in available open-source reporting. All charging documents describe financially motivated criminal extortion, not intelligence-directed operations. The victim profile lacks the targeting patterns typical of intelligence-value collection, and the UNDP attack is consistent with opportunistic financial targeting.

CIS Exclusion as Geopolitical Indicator

The Cyrillic-language kill switch (confirmed by Cisco Talos) combined with zero documented CIS victims is consistent with the informal "safe harbor" norm among Russia-based cybercriminals: avoid domestic and allied-state targets to reduce domestic-prosecution risk. HHS HC3 noted this exclusion "is a hallmark for many Russian-speaking threat actors" while stating no known correlation to Russia was established. The pattern does not by itself establish a state nexus or intelligence-sharing relationship.

Named Individuals and the Website-Developer Angle
Credible — single investigative source; subject denies involvement

A September 2023 KrebsOnSecurity investigation (with SentinelOne) linked the 8Base leak-site code to Andrei Kolev, a full-stack developer in Chisinau, Moldova, associated with a firm called JCube Group. The site was reportedly running in development mode, leaking a true IP (a Hetzner server in Finland) and a private GitLab repository path referencing an "8base-v2" project; the site's KYC/journalist-vetting language matched repository code. Kolev denied knowledge of the connection, and within minutes of being contacted the leak site changed its error handling. This introduces a possible Moldova-based developer distinct from the Russian operators later arrested, and is best treated as a credible but single-source lead, not a confirmed attribution.

Nationality and Intelligence Gaps
  • All named arrested individuals are Russian nationals; Berezhnoy and Glebov were in Thailand at arrest, Ptitsyn in South Korea — consistent with operating outside Russian jurisdiction to reduce arrest risk.
  • Unknown: whether any arrested individual had prior contact with Russian domestic law enforcement or intelligence.
  • Unknown: whether ransom-payment data was ever shared with or accessible to Russian state actors.
  • No documented safe-harbor relationship is found in available sources.
