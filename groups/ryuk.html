01

Executive Summary and Group Overview

Overall Assessment
Confirmed

Ryuk was one of the most financially destructive big game hunting (BGH) ransomware operations of its era, run by the Russia-based criminal enterprise that CrowdStrike tracks as WIZARD SPIDER and Secureworks tracks as GOLD ULRICK. First observed in August 2018, Ryuk was a heavily modified derivative of the commercially sold Hermes ransomware, deployed manually against high-value enterprise, government, and healthcare targets after initial access supplied by the TrickBot and Emotet ecosystems.

Two characteristics distinguished Ryuk from its RaaS contemporaries: it operated without a public dedicated leak site (DLS), and it did not exfiltrate data for publication leverage. Extortion relied purely on encryption and operational disruption. The Ryuk brand ceased around May 2021 as its operators fully transitioned to Conti, which shut down in June 2022 after the ContiLeaks. The core operator network subsequently dispersed across multiple successor operations. The lineage is defunct in name only: the personnel, tooling, and laundering infrastructure carried forward.

As of July 2026, the intelligence picture has advanced materially beyond the original operational period. In May 2025, German authorities named the enterprise leader "Stern" as Russian national Vitaly Nikolaevich Kovalev, and in July 2025 the U.S. unsealed the first named indictment of alleged Ryuk deployment operators (Vardanyan and co-conspirators), partially closing a long-standing attribution gap.

02

Lineage and Organizational Heritage

Hermes Ransomware Origin
Confirmed

Ryuk is a heavily modified derivative of Hermes, a ransomware kit sold on Russian-language forums (notably Exploit.in) by a Russian-speaking actor as early as August 2017, before North Korea's deployment of Hermes against Far Eastern International Bank (FEIB) in Taiwan in October 2017. Check Point Research identified significant code overlap between Ryuk and Hermes 2.x in August 2018.

CrowdStrike, FireEye, Kryptos Logic, and McAfee independently assessed in January 2019 that code similarity does not support a North Korean nexus. The operators purchased or obtained the Hermes source kit from the criminal market and substantially modified it. Confidence is high for Hermes code heritage and high against North Korean state attribution.

Conti Successor Relationship: Evidentiary Pillars

Multiple independent lines of evidence (on-chain, code-based, operational, and personnel) support the assessment that Conti is a rebrand and successor of Ryuk, operated by the same core Wizard Spider group. Anchor relationship confidence: high.

Pillar 1 • Confirmed
On-Chain Salary Trace
TRM Labs (April 2022) traced funds used to pay Conti team salaries in July 2021 (a payment from "Stern" to team lead "Mango," roughly $85,000) back to a known Ryuk ransom address reported by CrowdStrike in January 2019. The strongest forensic link under unified operator control.
Pillar 2 • Credible
Code Similarity
Conti and Ryuk share significant codebase elements. CertiK on-chain analysis (2023) found wallets for both groups active outside their nominal windows. Secureworks assesses GOLD ULRICK as the exclusive distributor (though not necessarily developer) of both payloads.
Pillar 3 • Confirmed
Operational Timeline
Ryuk paused February 2020; Conti appeared December 2019 to early 2020; Ryuk resumed September 2020 while Conti ran in parallel; Ryuk ceased around May 2021. Sequential and overlapping operation consistent with a managed transition.
Pillar 4 • Confirmed
Operator Continuity
"Stern," a senior Wizard Spider leader, is connected to both GOLD BLACKBURN and GOLD ULRICK chat infrastructure per the ContiLeaks. In May 2025 Stern was named as Vitaly Kovalev by German police (see Section 08).
Conflicting assessment: Some researchers framed Conti and Ryuk as separate entities under one umbrella group rather than a direct rebrand, citing the parallel operation period (late 2020 to early 2021) as co-existence rather than pure succession. Secureworks addresses this as "same operators, different tool," acknowledging that transient operators contributed to TTP variation during the Conti era.
Vendor Designation Disambiguation
Critical rule: WIZARD SPIDER (CrowdStrike) and GOLD ULRICK (Secureworks) designate the parent Conti/TrickBot criminal organization, not Ryuk as a standalone group. Do not conflate parent-organization designations with group-specific designations.

Operational Model

Structure: Non-Public Quasi-RaaS

Ryuk was not openly advertised as a Ransomware-as-a-Service operation. Unlike REvil or Maze, there was no public affiliate panel, dark web advertising, or posted affiliate terms. Secureworks CTU assessed that operator recruitment occurred through underground forum posts, existing trust relationships, and advertisements on legitimate job sites, including the Russian job portal hh[.]ru as revealed in the ContiLeaks.

TRM Labs analysis of the ContiLeaks (the same organizational infrastructure that preceded Conti and ran Ryuk) found a wage-based employee model supplementing a percentage-based affiliate model, an unusual structure that enabled industrial-scale operations. Average threat-actor salaries were roughly $2,000 USD per month. Note: this wage-model detail derives from Conti-era leaks applied retrospectively to the Ryuk period (moderate confidence).

Two-Tier Access and Target Selection
  • GOLD BLACKBURN (TrickBot/BazarLoader operators) ran mass spam campaigns to build inventories of infected hosts.
  • GOLD ULRICK (Ryuk operators) selectively identified high-value targets from those pools and manually deployed Ryuk.

This big game hunting model concentrated effort on high-revenue targets: large enterprises, government, and healthcare systems with low tolerance for disruption. Ryuk required direct access to TrickBot's Backconnect proxy module for interactive host access, restricting deployment to highly trusted operators with backend infrastructure access.

Decryptor bug: The operator-supplied decryptor mis-calculated the footer on large partially-encrypted files, truncating VHD/VHDX and database files and destroying the encrypted originals in the process. Even victims who paid could lose large files. This is a data-integrity liability, not a recovery opportunity for defenders.
CIS Exclusion Behavior

Early 2018 Ryuk samples included hardcoded path and language exclusions consistent with CIS/former-Soviet targets, standard practice for Russian-origin ransomware. Ryuk did not implement a CIS keyboard-layout kill switch in the manner of REvil. ANSSI's 2021 worm-variant analysis noted the new variant lacked any exclusion mechanism, including a re-infection mutex, meaning it could re-encrypt already-processed hosts, complicating containment. No systematic targeting of CIS entities has been documented.

Self-Propagating Worm Variant (2021)
Confirmed

In February 2021, ANSSI identified a Ryuk variant with worm-like self-propagation during incident response. It used a privileged domain account, enumerated IP addresses from the local ARP cache, sent Wake-on-LAN packets, mounted network shares, copied a unique executable (rep.exe or lan.exe) to each, and created remote scheduled tasks via schtasks.exe to execute the payload. It spread over Windows RPC rather than being limited to established lateral movement. Critically, it lacked a re-infection exclusion, so it could re-encrypt already-compromised devices. The HHS HC3 issued an analyst note on this variant on March 12, 2021.

05

Financial Infrastructure

Payment Model and Wallet Structure

Ryuk accepted Bitcoin exclusively, with no documented use of Monero. Demands were denominated in USD but paid in BTC. AdvIntel and HYAS (January 2021) traced payments across 61 Bitcoin addresses linked to Ryuk, estimating total earnings of at least $150 million USD. Victims typically paid through a third-party broker rather than directly to operator wallets; funds then moved to mixing and layering services before cash-out.

Chainalysis (January 2020) identified Huobi (Seychelles) and Binance as endpoints where laundered Ryuk proceeds were converted to fiat, describing a shadow network exploiting weak AML/KYC controls, in some cases using suspected stolen identities.

Laundering Methodology (Dubnikov Prosecution)

The Dubnikov prosecution documents (DOJ, 2023) provide the most detailed public picture of Ryuk laundering mechanics.

1
Receipt
Ransom received in Bitcoin via anonymous wallet addresses listed in ransom notes.
2
Layering
BTC split into smaller portions and forwarded to multiple wallets to obscure the trail.
3
Exchange placement
Funds placed into exchange accounts for conversion to other currencies.
4
Stablecoin conversion
Converted to Tether (USDT) and transferred to co-conspirators.
5
Fiat off-ramp
Co-conspirators exchanged USDT for Chinese Renminbi (CNY) via daisy-chained international transactions to obscure source and control.

Denis Dubnikov's OTC exchange Eggchange, based in Federation Tower East (Vostok) in Moscow, received over $34 million in cryptocurrency from darknet markets, scams, fraud shops, and ransomware operators between 2019 and 2021, per Chainalysis.

On-Chain Vendor Coverage
  • TRM Labs (April 2022): Confirmed the Stern-to-Mango salary payment traced to a January 2019 Ryuk address, the strongest Ryuk-to-Conti financial link (CONFIRMED).
  • Chainalysis (January 2020): Documented the Huobi/Binance cash-out network and the Ryuk broker payment pattern (CONFIRMED).
  • Elliptic: No dedicated Ryuk-specific on-chain report identified in open sources (gap; TRM and Chainalysis are the primary blockchain-forensics sources for Ryuk).
OFAC Sanctions

No OFAC designations were issued specifically against wallets or operators using the Ryuk brand. Sanctions targeting the parent enterprise came in February 2023 (see Section 07), when the U.S. and UK jointly sanctioned seven individuals for Conti, Ryuk, and TrickBot activity.

Victim Profile and Targeting

Scale and Sectors

Estimated total extraction exceeds $150 million through 2021 (AdvIntel/HYAS, 61 wallets); FBI reported $61.26 million in verified payments through October 2019 alone. SonicWall Capture Labs attributed roughly one-third of all 2020 ransomware incidents to Ryuk. No single authoritative victim count is public.

  • Healthcare / public health: most intensively targeted, especially during COVID-19 (2020). FBI assessed Ryuk had more completed payments than any other ransomware worldwide.
  • Government / municipal: at least 32 confirmed government entity victims (StateScoop).
  • Media / publishing: Tribune Publishing (December 2018) was the highest-profile early attack.
  • Financial services, technology, manufacturing, academia, MSPs, schools, ports: all documented.

Geographic Focus and Targeting Criteria

Primarily the United States, with secondary targeting of Western Europe (UK, France, Ireland, Germany). No documented systematic targeting of Russia, Belarus, or other CIS states. Observed selection criteria: high operational dependency on IT (hospitals, newspapers, government), perceived ability to pay large ransoms, and maximum urgency from disruption. The group showed no publicly documented restraint against healthcare during the pandemic.

07

Law Enforcement and Regulatory Response

2025 Ryuk Deployment Operator Indictment (Post-Cutoff)
Confirmed

In July 2025, the U.S. District Court for the District of Oregon unsealed charges against alleged Ryuk deployment operators, the first named indictment of Ryuk deployment personnel (as opposed to money launderers).

  • Karen Serobovich Vardanyan (Armenian, 33): extradited from Ukraine and arrived in the US on June 18, 2025; pleaded not guilty June 20. Charged with conspiracy, computer fraud, and computer extortion. Faces up to 5 years and a $250,000 fine per count.
  • Co-conspirators: Ukrainian nationals Oleg Nikolayevich Lyulyava (53) and Andrii Leonydovich Prykhodchenko (53), both at large; Armenian national Levon Georgiyovych Avetisyan (45), awaiting a US extradition request in France.
  • Conduct: Between March 2019 and September 2020, the group allegedly deployed Ryuk on hundreds of compromised servers and received about 1,160 BTC (over $15 million at the time).
  • Lead origin: Suspect identified via forensic analysis of equipment seized in a November 2023 multinational raid (Ukraine, US, France, Norway, Netherlands, Germany, plus Europol and Eurojust).
Gap closure: This indictment partially closes the long-standing intelligence gap that no Ryuk operators had been named for ransomware deployment. Prior public actions targeted only money laundering.
Money-Laundering Prosecution: Dubnikov

Denis Mihaqlovic Dubnikov (co-founder of Coyote Crypto and Eggchange) was charged August 2021 (District of Oregon), arrested in Amsterdam November 2021, extradited August 2022, pleaded guilty February 7, 2023 to conspiracy to commit money laundering, and was sentenced to time served with $2,000 restitution in April 2023. DOJ alleged the broader conspiracy laundered at least $70 million in Ryuk proceeds. The indictment references unnamed co-conspirators, the largest with laundering exceeding $35 million between roughly February 2020 and July 2021.

OFAC Sanctions (February 2023)

On February 9, 2023, the US Treasury and the UK jointly sanctioned seven Russian nationals for roles in spreading Conti, Ryuk, and TrickBot: Vitaliy Kovalev, Valery Sedletski, Valentin Karyagin, Maksim Mikhailov, Dmitry Pleshevskiy, Mikhail Iskritskiy, and Ivan Vakhromeyev. Travel bans and asset freezes were imposed. Kovalev was later identified as the leader "Stern" (see Section 08).

Government Advisories and Infrastructure Actions
  • Oct 28, 2020, Joint Advisory AA20-302A (FBI/CISA/HHS): warned of an "increased and imminent" Ryuk/TrickBot threat to healthcare, triggered by six US hospitals compromised in a 24-hour window.
  • May 2019, FBI TLP:WHITE Alert: early IOC release for Ryuk.
  • TrickBot disruption (Microsoft/ESET, October 2020): degraded the access infrastructure supporting Ryuk but did not take down Ryuk-specific C2. No Ryuk-specific infrastructure seizure is documented.
  • Operation Endgame (2024 to 2025): the multinational malware-infrastructure crackdown under which the Kovalev identification and related actions advanced.

Attribution and State Nexus

Assessment
Credible

No confirmed nexus to Russian state intelligence (FSB, SVR, GRU) has been established for Ryuk operations specifically. The group is assessed as financially motivated criminal actors, not state-directed or state-tasked. Several indicators are nonetheless relevant.

IndicatorFinding

CIS exclusionEarly samples carried CIS language/path exclusions; the 2021 worm variant lacked them

Named Leadership: "Stern" = Vitaly Kovalev (Post-Cutoff)
Credible to Confirmed

In May 2025, the enterprise leader known by the alias "Stern," connected on-chain and in chat logs to both Ryuk and Conti, was named as Vitaly Nikolaevich Kovalev, a 36-year-old Russian national. The leaker GangExposed published the identification on May 5, 2025; Germany's BKA confirmed it later that month as part of Operation Endgame and issued an Interpol red notice. Kovalev was already the first-listed individual in the February 2023 OFAC action, tying the leadership identity, the sanction, and the "Stern" alias together. Germany assesses he currently resides in Russia.

Confidence note: Government naming (German BKA) supports high confidence in the Kovalev = Stern identification. The initial disclosure originated with GangExposed, whose methodology remains partly unverified; the German confirmation is the load-bearing source.
Analyst Judgment
Analyst Inference

The combination of Russia-based operations, implicit non-prosecution safe harbor, target selection aligned with Russian geopolitical interests, and the successor organization's 2022 loyalty statement is consistent with a "tolerant state nexus": Russian services permit the group to operate freely in exchange for occasional cooperation or access, without direct operational tasking. No specific intelligence-sharing agreement, FSB tasking, or officer-level contact has been publicly confirmed for Ryuk. Any stronger nexus claim would require classified reporting.

Trajectory Assessment

Historical Phases
  • Phase 1 (Aug 2018 to Jan 2020): rapid build-up. Revenue grew from an estimated $640,000 in the first four months to $61.26 million by October 2019.
  • Phase 2 (Feb to Aug 2020): apparent pause; Conti emerged as a parallel or successor platform.
  • Phase 3 (Sep 2020 to May 2021): aggressive resurgence focused on US healthcare during COVID-19; ZeroLogon adoption enabling sub-5-hour domain compromise; the self-propagating worm variant (Feb 2021); final cessation around May 2021.
Connected Group Cluster

Two-tier confidence applies: the anchor relationships (TrickBot, Emotet, Conti) are strongly corroborated; the post-Conti fragment attributions rest more heavily on single primary sources.

Trajectory Indicators and Intelligence Gaps

The Ryuk brand is permanently defunct; there is no indication of revival under the Ryuk name. The relevant trajectory is that of the successor cluster and its named leadership. The February 2022 ContiLeaks exposed the full Wizard Spider organizational architecture, and Operation Endgame plus the 2025 indictments and the Kovalev identification represent sustained pressure on surviving operators. Wizard Spider is assessed as disbanded as of open-source reporting through 2024, with individual operators dispersed.

  • Remaining gaps: most co-conspirators in the Dubnikov and Vardanyan cases remain unidentified or at large; the original Ryuk codebase author is unidentified; the specific current activities of core operators are not documented.
  • Partially closed: named Ryuk deployment operators (2025 indictment) and named enterprise leadership (Stern = Kovalev, 2025).
