01

Executive Summary & Group Overview

ALPHV/BlackCat was a Russian-speaking Ransomware-as-a-Service operation that ran continuously from November 2021 to March 2024. It is widely assessed as the third-generation evolution of a single core criminal operation: DarkSide, then BlackMatter, then ALPHV/BlackCat. At its peak it was the second most prolific RaaS variant globally by victim count and is credited by the U.S. Department of Justice with extorting hundreds of millions of dollars.

The operation ended in a self-inflicted collapse. In December 2023 the FBI disclosed a months-long infiltration of ALPHV's backend and distributed a decryption tool to more than 500 victims. In February 2024 an affiliate breached Change Healthcare (UnitedHealth Group); in early March 2024 UnitedHealth paid approximately $22 million in Bitcoin, and the ALPHV operators kept the entire payment, defrauding the responsible affiliate and ceasing operations. RansomHub subsequently absorbed the displaced affiliate base.

02

Lineage & Organizational Heritage

Assessed Predecessor Chain: DarkSide to BlackMatter to ALPHV
Credible to Confirmed • multi-vendor and government consensus

The consensus assessment across multiple vendors and government advisories is that ALPHV/BlackCat represents the third generation of a single core operation. DarkSide launched August 2020 and shut down May 2021 after the Colonial Pipeline attack drew international law-enforcement pressure. BlackMatter relaunched 31 July 2021 and closed in November 2021 after Emsisoft exploited a cryptographic weakness to build a decryptor and servers were seized. ALPHV/BlackCat emerged in November 2021, immediately after BlackMatter's closure.

The Canadian Centre for Cyber Security assessed that ALPHV users and source code are related to BlackMatter and DarkSide. Secureworks CTU assessed with moderate confidence that BlackCat was a rebrand of BlackMatter, which in turn evolved from DarkSide.

Evidentiary Pillars
Pillar 1 • High
Timing
ALPHV emerged within weeks of BlackMatter's November 2021 shutdown, filling the gap left by the seized operation.
Pillar 2 • High
Self-Admission
ALPHV told The Record they were adverts of darkmatter (DarkSide/BlackMatter) who suffered from Emsisoft's decryptor. A direct statement, though operators may be distancing from core membership.
Pillar 3 • Moderate
Operational Continuity
Feature and configuration-file overlap despite an entirely new Rust codebase. Emsisoft's Fabian Wosar noted structural similarity even though the language change erased code-level similarity.
Pillar 4 • Moderate
Affiliate Continuity
DEV-0237 and DEV-0504 previously deployed BlackMatter and immediately adopted ALPHV. LockBit's representative publicly called ALPHV a DarkSide/BlackMatter rebrand (competitor claim).
Disputed Position: Operators versus Affiliates
Credible • single-vendor assessment (Emsisoft)

Brett Callow (Emsisoft) argued ALPHV is more likely the DarkSide/BlackMatter operators, not merely former affiliates, who rebuilt under a new brand after the decryptor loss damaged BlackMatter and cost affiliates millions. ALPHV publicly claimed to be only former affiliates. Callow's read is assessed as more likely given timing and behavioral continuity, but it cannot be proven absent attribution of specific individuals. Broader vendor consensus does not definitively resolve the affiliate-versus-operator distinction.

Vendor Designation Disambiguation

ALPHV has no Conti lineage, so the Wizard Spider (CrowdStrike) and Gold Ulrick (Secureworks) parent-Conti designations do not apply. For ALPHV specifically: CrowdStrike uses ALPHA SPIDER (operators) and SCATTERED SPIDER (a key affiliate); Secureworks uses GOLD BLAZER; Microsoft names affiliates (Pistachio Tempest, Velvet Tempest, Octo Tempest); Symantec uses the Noberus malware family name; Mandiant uses ALPHV plus UNC4466 for the Veritas-exploitation affiliate.

03

Operational Model

RaaS Structure

ALPHV operated a classic RaaS model. Core developers maintained the malware, infrastructure, negotiation panels, and leak site. Affiliates (internally, adverts) conducted intrusions, lateral movement, data exfiltration, and payload deployment.

Affiliate Recruitment & Revenue Split
  • Recruitment via private forums XSS, Exploit, and RAMP5. A high technical bar is implied by the caliber of participating affiliates (Scattered Spider, FIN12/DEV-0237).
  • Standard split: 80% affiliate / 20% operator; raised to 90% for affiliates after the December 2023 disruption to retain them.
  • The DOJ prosecution of affiliates Goldberg, Martin, and Martino confirmed the 20% operator share in exchange for access to the ransomware and extortion platform.

Data Publication & the Searchable Portal

ALPHV published stolen data from non-paying victims on a dedicated leak site and pioneered a searchable stolen-data portal that let anyone (including a victim's customers and partners) query their own exposed information, sharply increasing pressure. Releases were staged around negotiation leverage points. After the December 2023 disruption, ALPHV explicitly removed policies restricting hospital and critical-infrastructure targeting as retaliation.

Persistence, Lateral Movement & Tooling
  • Rogue accounts (e.g. aadmin) for persistent domain access; Windows Task Scheduler plus malicious GPOs for deployment.
  • Lateral movement via PsExec self-propagation, SMB, RDP, and ADRecon; Kerberos token generation (T1558).
  • Offensive tooling: Cobalt Strike, Brute Ratel C4, Metasploit, Mimikatz, LaZagne, Nanodump.
  • Remote access: AnyDesk, Splashtop, ScreenConnect/ConnectWise, Plink, Ngrok; Evilginx2 for MFA and session-cookie theft.
  • POORTRY / STONESTOP drivers for EDR termination (linked to Scattered Spider / UNC3944).
  • Exfiltration: MEGAsync, Rclone (renamed as legitimate Windows processes), MEGA.nz, Dropbox.
Encryption Implementation
  • Language: Rust, purpose-built. The first major ransomware written in Rust.
  • Algorithms: AES (hardware-accelerated) with ChaCha20 fallback where AES-NI is unavailable.
  • Modes: four configurable options (full, fast, auto, smart), set per affiliate.
  • Cross-platform: Windows, Linux (Debian, Ubuntu, ReadyNAS, Synology), VMware ESXi.
  • Recovery inhibition: vssadmin/wmic shadow-copy deletion; wevtutil log clearing; UAC bypass via dllhost.exe.
  • Ransom note: RECOVER-[7-digit extension]-FILES.txt (AA23-353A).
  • Sphynx (v2.0), Feb 2023: improved defense evasion, embedded lateral-movement tooling, enhanced cross-platform reach.
CIS Exclusion Behavior
Analyst Inference • Intelligence gap

The Canadian Centre for Cyber Security assessed ALPHV operators are very likely CIS-based, and CIS-country victims were nominally excluded. Public technical reporting does not confirm a binary-level CIS keyboard-layout kill switch in the ALPHV encryptor (unlike DarkSide/BlackMatter). The exclusion appears to be an affiliate-management policy rather than a hard-coded restriction. Analysts should consult Symantec (Noberus) and Emsisoft analyses for definitive confirmation.

Decryptors

The FBI built a decryption tool during the December 2023 operation, derived from 946 public/private key pairs obtained via covert access, and distributed it to 500+ victims, avoiding an estimated $68M (initial reporting) to $99M (DOJ final figure) in ransoms. The tool was distributed through FBI field offices rather than posted publicly; no third-party decryptor for post-Sphynx variants is documented on No More Ransom or vendor sites as of the profile date.

05

Financial Infrastructure

Payment Model
  • Bitcoin is the confirmed rail for all major documented payments: $22M (Change Healthcare), $1.27M (Goldberg/Martin case), and others.
  • Monero: some reporting suggests possible acceptance for anonymity-sensitive transactions, but ALPHV-specific Monero usage is unconfirmed in reviewed primary sources and should be verified against TRM Labs / Chainalysis private reporting.
On-Chain Laundering

DOJ filings confirm that after a $1.27M payment, affiliates split their 80% share three ways and laundered it through various means; specific techniques (mixers, bridges, exchanges) were not detailed in public court documents. Chainalysis identified ALPHV/BlackCat among 2023's top-grossing strains and noted its early-2024 exit scam left a void in the ransomware payment landscape. Detailed ALPHV-specific wallet tracing has not been published in reviewed public Chainalysis reports.

On-Chain Successor Signal: Embargo
CREDIBLE (TRM Labs, Aug 2025): TRM traced roughly $34.2M in crypto to Embargo, a RaaS that surfaced in 2024, and assessed Embargo may be a rebrand or successor of BlackCat/ALPHV. Anchor evidence: historical BlackCat-linked addresses funneled funds into wallet clusters tied to Embargo victims. Laundering routes through intermediary wallets, high-risk exchanges, and the sanctioned platform Cryptex.net, with roughly $18.8M held dormant in unattributed wallets. See Section 09.
OFAC Sanctions

No OFAC designations against ALPHV/BlackCat wallets or named operators appear in reviewed public SDN entries as of the profile date. Sealed or undisclosed designations, and private analytics data, cannot be excluded. Intelligence gap.

Sector, Geography & Size

Victim selection was assessed as almost certainly opportunity-based (Canadian Centre for Cyber Security). High-frequency sectors: healthcare (most heavily targeted after the December 2023 disruption), finance and insurance, technology, legal, energy, manufacturing, hospitality/gaming, government, and education. Geography: the large majority of confirmed victims were in the United States, followed by Canada, Australia, and Western Europe; Microsoft observed additional impact across Africa, Asia, and Latin America. Size: most victims were small and medium organizations, though large enterprises (MGM, Caesars, Change Healthcare) were compromised.

Targeting Exclusions (policy level)

ALPHV's stated affiliate policy nominally prohibited attacks on government, healthcare, and education. These restrictions were violated in practice and were explicitly removed by the operators in December 2023 in retaliation for the FBI disruption.

07

Law Enforcement & Regulatory Response

Infrastructure Disruption, December 2023
  • FBI signed up a confidential source as an ALPHV affiliate, obtaining backend panel access and silently monitoring for months.
  • FBI seized 946 public/private key pairs operating ALPHV's Tor infrastructure (affiliate panels, breach sites, victim comms).
  • Decryption tool distributed to 500+ victims; seizure banner placed on the leak site. Partners: Europol and agencies in Denmark, Germany, Australia, Spain, Austria, Netherlands, and the UK; coordinated with USAO SDFL and DOJ CCIPS.
  • ALPHV response: within hours claimed to unseize its domain, moved infrastructure, and threatened attacks on hospitals and critical infrastructure, demonstrating rapid reconstitution.
Criminal Charges: Affiliate Prosecution (S.D. Florida)
Post-cutoff update (2026): the originating draft listed sentencing as pending (scheduled 12 March 2026, 20-year maximum) and the third co-conspirator as unnamed. Updated status: Ryan Clifford Goldberg and Kevin Tyler Martin were each sentenced to four years in federal prison. The third co-conspirator is Angelo Martino, 41, of Land O' Lakes, Florida, a former DigitalMint negotiator, who pleaded guilty in April 2026 with sentencing set for 9 July 2026. Total losses across the affiliate group's victims exceeded $9.5M.

Charge: conspiracy to obstruct commerce by extortion (Hobbs Act, 18 U.S.C. 1951(a)). Between April and December 2023 the three obtained an ALPHV affiliate account and attacked U.S. victims (a California doctor's office and engineering firm, a Florida medical-device company, a Maryland pharmaceutical company, and a Virginia drone manufacturer), extorting $1.27M in Bitcoin from one victim. DOJ highlighted that the defendants were working cybersecurity professionals who weaponized specialized knowledge.

Scattered Spider (separate affiliate prosecution)

In November 2024 DOJ charged five individuals tied to Scattered Spider (Tyler Buchanan, Noah Urban, Ahmed Elbadawy, Evans Osiebo, Joel Evans) with hacking, fraud, and identity theft. These are affiliate-level charges, not ALPHV operator charges.

No Named Core Operators; Sanctions
As of profile date: no ALPHV core operator (developer or administrator) has been publicly named or charged, and no OFAC sanctions target named ALPHV operators or wallets. The exit-scam destination of the $22M is undisclosed. DOJ noted potential eligibility for the State Department Transnational Organized Crime Rewards and Rewards for Justice programs.
08

Attribution & State Nexus

Assessed Jurisdiction
Credible • Russia / CIS

The Canadian Centre for Cyber Security assessed ALPHV as almost certainly a financially motivated, Russian-speaking RaaS group very likely based in a CIS country. This is a geographic and linguistic assessment, corroborated by FBI/CISA/HHS descriptions of the group as Russian-speaking. It is not a state-direction assessment.

Russian Intelligence Services Nexus
Analyst Inference • No confirmed RIS nexus

No confirmed nexus to Russian state intelligence (FSB, SVR, GRU) is established in public reporting. The no-nexus assessment rests on absence of positive evidence rather than confirmed negative evidence: no court documents, declassified assessments, or vetted vendor reports reviewed allege state direction of the ALPHV core operators.

  • The nominal (non-binary-enforced) CIS exclusion is consistent with informal safe-harbor norms for CIS-based cybercrime, but is not evidence of state coordination.
  • Operational behavior (RaaS commercialization, exit scam, affiliate disputes) is consistent with a purely financially motivated enterprise. The exit scam, defrauding its own affiliates of $22M, is inconsistent with a state-directed operation where reputational stability matters.
Confidence: moderate confidence in no confirmed state nexus. This does not preclude passive tolerance, non-prosecution safe harbor, or incidental intelligence sharing that has not been publicly documented.
Named Individuals

None at the operator level. All named, charged individuals to date are affiliates (Goldberg, Martin, Martino) or affiliate-collective members (Scattered Spider defendants). Operator identities remain unconfirmed.

09

Trajectory Assessment

Defunct. ALPHV/BlackCat ceased operations in March 2024 via an exit scam. Continuity persists at two layers: the affiliate base (absorbed largely by RansomHub) and possibly the operator/tooling layer (Embargo, CREDIBLE).
Collapse Sequence
I
Dec 2023 • FBI Infiltration
Severe OPSEC failure; decryptor distribution damaged revenue and affiliate confidence despite rapid reconstitution within hours.
II
Dec 2023 • Healthcare-Targeting Escalation
Post-disruption removal of hospital-targeting restrictions was reputationally destabilizing and increased law-enforcement attention without commensurate revenue.
III
Mar 2024 • Exit Scam
Operators kept the full $22M Change Healthcare payment, withholding the ~$17.6M (80%) owed to the responsible affiliate and breaking the core RaaS trust mechanism. A self-authored fake FBI seizure notice was placed on the site to mask the exit.
IV
Mar 2024 • Dissolution
Reported attempt to sell the ransomware source code for ~$5M as a final monetization play. The defrauded affiliate passed Change Healthcare's data to RansomHub, which mounted a second extortion attempt.
Connected Group Cluster (two-tier confidence)

Per method, the anchor relationship is assessed separately from extension and interpretation claims.

RansomHub (successor ecosystem) • Anchor CONFIRMED: RansomHub emerged February 2024 as ALPHV collapsed, listed Change Healthcare's stolen data, and its administrators confirmed to Vx-Underground that former BlackCat affiliates were joining. Its affiliate-first 90/10 model appears designed to remedy the trust deficit ALPHV's exit scam created. SOCRadar and others assess RansomHub as a distinct operation that absorbed the affiliate base rather than an ALPHV rebrand (its leak site predated ALPHV's exit).
Scattered Spider (affiliate) • Anchor CONFIRMED: documented by CrowdStrike (SCATTERED SPIDER), Microsoft (Octo Tempest), Mandiant (UNC3944), Trend Micro, and GuidePoint. Executed the September 2023 MGM and Caesars attacks using ALPHV; shared POORTRY/STONESTOP drivers. GuidePoint assessed with high confidence that some members migrated to RansomHub after the collapse.
Pistachio Tempest (DEV-0237 / FIN12) & Velvet Tempest (DEV-0504) • Anchor CONFIRMED (Microsoft): DEV-0237 adopted BlackCat from March 2022 (prior: Ryuk, Conti, Hive); DEV-0504 adopted BlackCat from December 2021 (prior: BlackMatter, Conti, LockBit 2.0, REvil, Ryuk).
Embargo (rebrand candidate) • Anchor CREDIBLE; rebrand interpretation ANALYST INFERENCE: TRM Labs (Aug 2025) assessed Embargo (surfaced 2024, ~$34.2M traced) may be a rebrand or successor of BlackCat, citing on-chain overlap (historical BlackCat addresses funneling into Embargo-linked clusters) plus off-chain similarity (Rust, similar encryption toolkits, similar leak-site design). Neither Mandiant nor Recorded Future has published a formal Embargo/ALPHV assessment; the rebrand interpretation is not yet corroborated.
DarkSide / BlackMatter (predecessors) • Lineage moderate-to-high; operator identity overlap likely but unproven: see Section 02. Assessments span Secureworks (moderate, rebrand), Emsisoft (high, same operators), Canadian Centre for Cyber Security (high, code and user relationship), and Symantec (successor).
Trajectory Indicators
Analyst Inference • Forward monitoring

ALPHV grew rapidly through 2023 to become the second most prolific RaaS by victim count before the FBI disruption. The disruption, followed immediately by the exit scam, effectively zeroed the operation; Chainalysis noted the exit left a void in the 2024 ransomware landscape. Monitoring priorities: Embargo on-chain corroboration by a second vendor; any operator-level indictment or OFAC action; and resurfacing of ALPHV source code or the searchable-portal innovation in successor operations.
